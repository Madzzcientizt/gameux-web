        Overview

          Core Gameplay Loops:

        • Emotion-Based Turn Combat
        • Dual-Reality Traversal
        • Psychological Trauma Encounters

        Omori is a surreal, story-driven psychological horror RPG Maker game developed by OMOCAT. Players take on the role of a silent protagonist traversing between two distinct realities: the vibrant, pastel dreamscape of "Headspace" and the muted, emotionally fraught reality of "Faraway Town".

        From an HCI perspective, Omori utilizes what can be termed as "Affective Computing Paradigms." Instead of abstract status ailments like 'Poison' or 'Paralyze', the game explicitly gamifies human emotion. The UI brilliantly reflects this by translating complex emotional states into functional rock-paper-scissors combat mechanics, while utilizing a heavily stylized "sketchbook" aesthetic to blur the lines between the protagonist's coping mechanisms and the user interface.

        • Cognitive Load
          Bifurcated
          Standard RPG load in Headspace, shifting to heavy narrative/psychological processing in the real world.
        • Interaction Frequency
          Turn-Based Tactical
          Requires deliberate navigation of menus to chain emotional states across party members.
        • Visual Aesthetics
          Ludonarrative UI
          Hand-drawn UI elements intentionally meant to represent the main character's internal mental state.

        UI Components

        [ ELEMENT_01 ] The Sketchbook UI

        The main pause menu completely abandons the clean, digital lines of traditional RPGs. Instead, it is framed as a literal notebook with crayon-scrawled text, imperfect borders, and shaky, animated line art. This grounds the interface diegetically within the protagonist's mind, acting as a child's coping mechanism.

          Functionality
        • Houses inventory ("Pockets") and skills
        • Visually separates the Dream World from Reality
        [ ELEMENT_02 ] The Emotion HUD

        During combat, the lower half of the screen displays detailed, hand-drawn portraits of the four party members. When an emotion is applied (e.g., Sad, Angry, Happy), the portrait immediately updates its expression and color-coding, providing instant, highly readable visual feedback on the character's status.

        [ ELEMENT_03 ] The AFRAID Status

        During specific, trauma-induced psychological battles, the UI weaponizes its own rules. When a character becomes "AFRAID," standard skills are greyed out, attack accuracy plummets, and the screen visually warps. The UI actively restricts player agency to simulate a panic attack.

        Emotional Logic & Cognitive Mapping

        Omori replaces standard elemental weaknesses (Fire beats Ice) with a cyclical system of emotions. For the player to succeed, they must map these human affective states to functional mathematical advantages.

        [ AFFECTIVE TRIANGLE ]
        Happy > Angry > Sad > Happy

        This system is brilliant UX because it leans on real-world logic. A 'Happy' character is fast and lucky, but an 'Angry' character deals massive damage while lowering their defense. A 'Sad' character ignores some damage but drains their MP. By using universal emotional shorthand, the UI minimizes the cognitive load required to memorize a complex elemental chart.

        Visual Hierarchy Breakdown

        Combat Interface Layering
        1. The Adversary (Center Top)

          First-person perspective places the enemy front and center. Enemy sprites utilize a "boiling line" animation style (wiggling lines) to give static 2D images a sense of nervous, kinetic energy.

        2. The Party State (Bottom)

          The emotional portraits of the party members. Players must constantly monitor this row, as shifting an enemy's emotion is useless if your own party's emotional state isn't synergized.

        3. Action Economy (Periphery)

          The "Energy" gauge. Accumulating energy allows for distinct "Follow-Up" attacks, encouraging rhythmic, sequential input chaining between specific characters.

        The game's use of color is critical. "Happy" is universally bright yellow/pink, "Angry" is harsh red, and "Sad" is deep blue. This color-coding bleeds into the text, the background, and the UI panels, ensuring the player is constantly aware of the emotional terrain.

        User Interaction

        A breakdown of the turn-based loop and how the game utilizes its distinct visual styles to communicate shifting realities.

        The Combat Loop

        1. Assess (Emotion)

          Check the current emotional state of the enemy and the party by reading the visual portraits and color cues.

        2. Manipulate (Shift)

          Use specific skills or items (e.g., "Mock," "Poetry Book") to inflict an emotion on the enemy that disadvantages them.

        3. Synergize (Match)

          Alter the party's own emotions to counter the enemy (e.g., making the party Happy if the enemy is Angry).

        4. Execute (Follow-Up)

          Unleash high-damage combos utilizing the built-up Energy meter, triggering unique, hand-drawn character interaction animations.

        Multi-Modal Feedback Matrix

        As a horror game, Omori relies heavily on sensory distortion. The interface will frequently break its own established rules to signal to the player that the protagonist's mental state is deteriorating.

        • Auditory Escalation

          In moments of high trauma, the chipper RPG music drops out entirely, replaced by the rhythmic, heavy thumping of a heartbeat or the abrasive sound of scribbling markers, directly tying audio feedback to physiological anxiety.

        • Visual Typography

          Dialogue text utilizes capitalization and screen shaking to denote volume and stress. When the "Something" phobias appear, the text box itself may warp or display unreadable, glitching characters to simulate dissociation.

        • Input Denial

          During critical narrative sequences, the game will present choices but physically refuse to let the player select the "safe" option. Forcing the cursor to select a traumatic memory is a stark subversion of RPG interface agency.

        HCI Principles

        The Dual Reality Interface

        Omori utilizes two distinct UI paradigms to enforce its narrative. When in the dream-like "Headspace," menus are bright purple, bouncing, and filled with whimsical terminology (Clams for money, Juice for MP). When waking up in the real world, the UI strips all this away.

          Real-World Grounding
        • Muted Colors: The real-world UI is stark black, white, and sepia, immediately signaling a drop in dopamine and emotional vibrancy.
        • Terminological Shift: "Juice" becomes standard RPG terminology, removing the protective layer of childhood imagination.

        Spatial Memory and Trauma Mapping

        The game plays heavily with spatial orientation. The layout of the protagonist's house is burned into the player's memory over several days. When the psychological horror elements activate, the game slightly alters this familiar spatial congruence€”adding a door that shouldn't exist, extending a hallway infinitely, or replacing the pause menu with a blank white void. This disruption of expected spatial mapping is the root of the game's creeping dread.

        Managing RPG Maker Clunkiness

        Because Omori is built on the RPG Maker engine, it inherently inherits some of the clunky, grid-based menu constraints of the software, which the developers had to creatively mask.

        Extrinsic Limitations Intrinsic Masking
        Inventory Bloat

        RPG Maker naturally lists every item in endless, scrolling text columns, which can become overwhelming mid-to-late game.

        Theming over Function

        By framing the inventory as "Pockets" filled with literal junk (rubber bands, sparklers), the messy UI feels somewhat intentionally juvenile.

        Status Opacity

        The exact mathematical percentages of how much "Angry" lowers defense vs. raises attack is never explicitly shown in the UI.

        Experiential Learning

        The game forces players to learn through trial and error, providing qualitative visual feedback (big damage numbers) rather than quantitative data menus.

        By turning human emotions into a highly readable, color-coded combat system, Omori brilliantly bridges the gap between traditional RPG mechanics and a deeply personal narrative about psychological processing.

        Strengths and Weaknesses Analysis

        Evaluating the friction between an incredibly stylized, emotionally resonant aesthetic and the structural limitations of its underlying game engine.

        System Strengths Positive_Vectors System Weaknesses Negative_Vectors
        01_LUDONARRATIVE
        Perfect Narrative Resonance

        The UI is not just a tool; it is the story. The sketchbook aesthetic and the emotional combat system perfectly align the player's mechanical actions with the protagonist's mental state.

        01_ENGINE_LIMITS
        Legacy Menu Navigation

        Due to the RPG Maker constraints, equipping items, checking sub-menus, and managing deep inventories can feel unnecessarily tedious and reliant on endless scrolling rather than modern grid layouts.

        02_AESTHETIC
        Visual Feedback Loops

        The hand-drawn, shifting portraits during combat provide instant, highly readable feedback that transcends language, making the complex emotional system incredibly easy to parse at a glance.

        02_OBSCURITY
        Hidden Mechanics

        The game obscures the secondary and tertiary tiers of emotions (e.g., Happy -> Ecstatic -> Manic) early on, leading to moments where players might accidentally over-buff an enemy without understanding the UI consequence.

        Omori stands as a benchmark for Affective User Interfaces in gaming. By framing its menus as a childhood sketchbook and translating psychological trauma into a brilliantly readable color-coded combat system, it proves that UI design can be as integral to storytelling as the dialogue itself.
